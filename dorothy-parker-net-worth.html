Dorothy Parker Full Overview and Wiki

Contribution to American Literature

Dorothy was not just a writer; she was a cultural icon of the roaring twenties. Known for her sharp, concise, and often brutally honest satirical poetry, she held up a mirror to early 20th-century American society.

Her works like Enough Rope and contributions to The New Yorker remain significant, showing her prowess in capturing the complexities of human emotions and societal norms.

Algonquin Round Table

At the famed Algonquin Round Table, a meeting place for New York’s literary and theatrical elite, Parker was a central figure.

Her exchanges there with contemporaries like Edna Ferber and Robert Benchley not only influenced her writing but also helped cement her status as a leading intellectual.

Satirical Wit on 20th-Century American Satire

Parker’s legacy in American satire is as sharp as her wit. Her biting humor and critical social commentary pushed the boundaries of what women could write about in the early 20th century. This not only opened doors for future generations of writers but also left an indelible mark on the genre of satire.

Hollywood Career and Screenwriting Achievements

Transitioning to Hollywood in the 1930s, Parker applied her literary talents to the silver screen, earning two Academy Award nominations. Her screenplays often explored complex characters and emotional depths, showcasing her versatility beyond mere humorous sketches.

Political Activism and the Hollywood Blacklist

Her sharp tongue and pen often landed her in hot water, most notably resulting in her name appearing on the Hollywood blacklist due to her outspoken left-wing political views. Despite this setback, she remained a fierce advocate for civil rights and free speech.

Legacy in Civil Rights and Feminism

Beyond literature, Parker was a vocal supporter of civil rights and an early advocate for women’s rights, themes that were often reflected in her writing. She used her platform to champion the causes she believed in, paving the way for activists that followed.

FAQs about Dorothy Parker

Who was Dorothy Parker?

She was an American poet, writer, critic, and satirist known for her sharp wit and acerbic remarks. She was a founding member of the Algonquin Round Table, an informal literary group.

When and where was Parker born?

She was born on August 22, 1893, in West End, near Long Beach, New Jersey, U.S..

What was Parker’s early life like?

She had a troubled childhood with the early death of her mother and stepmother, and she detested her stepmother and father. She grew up on Manhattan’s Upper West Side and had her education ended abruptly at fourteen.

How did Parker start her career?

She sold her first poem to Vanity Fair in 1914. By the age of twenty-two, she was working at Vogue, and she later joined Vanity Fair as a drama critic, replacing P.G. Wodehouse.

What is Dorothy Parker known for?

She is known for her literary works that included poetry, short stories, and critiques, as well as her participation in the Algonquin Round Table. Her works were known for their wit and critique of society.

Did Parker have any significant relationships?

She married Edwin Pond Parker II in 1917, but the marriage was tumultuous, leading to their divorce in 1928. She later married Alan Campbell, with whom she moved to Hollywood to work as a screenwriter.

What awards and recognitions did Dorothy Parker receive?

Parker won the O. Henry Award in 1929 for her short story Big Blonde. She was inducted into the American Academy of Arts and Letters in 1959.

What were some of Parker’s most notable works?

Her notable works include Enough Rope, Sunset Gun, Death and Taxes, and Big Blonde, among others.

What was Parker’s involvement in politics?

She became a socialist in 1927, influenced by the Sacco and Vanzetti trial. She was also called before the House Un-American Activities Committee (HUAC) in 1955.

How and when did Dorothy Parker die?

She died of a heart attack on June 6, 1967, in a New York City hotel. She was 73 years old.
